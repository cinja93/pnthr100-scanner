1. What Ambush is, in one paragraph

Ambush is an automated trading engine. It watches a fixed list of about 300 stocks (the "AI 300"), and it only buys or sells when three separate conditions line up at the same moment: a longer-term trend is in place, a short-term breakout is happening today, and the price has just popped through a precise trigger this hour. When all three agree, it takes a position in the direction of the trend — buying stocks that are breaking out upward (a "long"), or short-selling stocks breaking down (a "short"). It starts small and adds more only as the trade proves it's working. It always carries a protective stop — an automatic exit order — that trails behind the price and tightens as the trade goes its way, so a winner can't turn back into a loser. It checks everything against your real brokerage account (Interactive Brokers, "IBKR") every single minute, and it treats that account as the absolute truth. It runs only while the U.S. market is open.

3. The heartbeat — how often it thinks
  • Once every 60 seconds, Monday through Friday, only while the market is open (9:30 AM to about 4:05 PM, New York time). Outside those hours, and on weekends, it does nothing.
  • Only one copy ever runs. A lock in the database guarantees that even if the system is running on several servers, exactly one of them acts each minute. There is no way to accidentally place an order twice.
  • It can be switched off with a single setting. If it's off, or it's outside market hours, the minute "tick" simply ends and does nothing.
  • Between ticks it remembers everything by saving its state to the database, so it survives restarts without losing track of any position.
4. The three green lights before it enters

Think of three filters stacked on top of each other. A stock has to pass all three at the same moment before Ambush will buy it. This is the heart of the whole system.

Green light 1 — the WEEKLY trend (the slow filter)

Once a week, Ambush looks at each stock's weekly chart and asks: is this a healthy uptrend? Using a proprietary sector-tuned exponential moving average (a smoothed trend line) and a proprietary PNTHR signal system, a stock earns a Buy-Long signal when its price has climbed above a rising trend line, broken above the prior two weeks' high, the slope and gap are correct, and it isn't stretched too far above the line (a healthy, not over-extended, advance). A Sell-Short signal is the exact mirror below the same conditions, just reversed. This weekly signal decides which handful of the 300 stocks are even allowed to trade today, and in which direction. It stays "on" until a weekly exit prints. Everything after this is about timing.

Green light 2 — the DAILY breakout (today's confirmation)

For a stock that has the weekly signal, Ambush then checks today's price against the last two daily bars: for a long, the live price must be above the higher of the previous two days' highs, plus a penny; for a short, below the lower of the previous two days' lows, minus a penny. That's the daily breakout. If the price slips back inside that two-day range, the breakout is no longer intact and the stock is not a candidate this minute.

Green light 3 — the HOURLY trigger (the exact moment)

The precise tripwire. Ambush takes the most recent completed hourly bar and waits for the live price to break above that bar's high by a penny (for a long) or below its low by a penny (for a short). The instant the price crosses — caught on the 60-second tick, not after waiting for a bar to close — that's the entry. The bar can be any color; all that matters is the break of its high (or low).

Two things to note. First, because the engine needs a completed hourly bar to break, the earliest any NEW entry can fire is after the first hour ends at 10:30 (the first completed bar is the 9:30–10:30 bar). Second, the trigger is always read in the direction the weekly signal set: if the weekly is a Buy-Long, the engine only looks for an upward break — never a short — and the daily breakout above is in that same upward direction too. The three lights always point the same way.

Two side-filters, and the direction rule. (1) A broad market-direction reading is calculated but is turned off in this version — Ambush takes both longs and shorts. (2) A sector filter that must agree with the trade's direction. Each of the AI sectors is rated BULL or BEAR by its own 5-day return — positive over the last five days is BULL, negative is BEAR (this is exactly the badge on the "Sectors AI" page). A long is only allowed in a BULL sector, a short only in a BEAR sector. So the full test to enter, all pointing the same way, is: weekly signal on + the stock's sector agrees (BULL for a long, BEAR for a short) + daily breakout intact + hourly trigger fires.
5. How big a bet it makes

The risk on any one trade

Before entering, Ambush measures the risk per share — how far the price would have to fall to hit the protective stop (for a long, that's the entry price minus the stop price). Then it picks the number of shares as the smallest of three limits, so no single rule is ever broken:

LimitThe mathWhat it protects
Dollar risk$300 ÷ risk-per-shareNever lose more than $300 on one trade if it hits the stop
1% of NAV(1% × NAV) ÷ risk-per-shareOne trade never risks more than 1% of the account
10% of NAV(10% × NAV) ÷ entry priceNo single stock is ever more than 10% of the account

That share count is then scaled by a growth dial tied to account size, so the risk stays proportional as the fund grows:

NAVDialEffect
Below $125K50%~$150 most you can lose on a trade — this is the current setting
$125K to $166K75%~$225 most you can lose
$166K and up100%the full $300 most you can lose

At today's account size the dial is at 50%, so the real most-you-can-lose per trade is about $150, not $300. It steps up only as the account grows.

The five-lot pyramid

The full share count is split into five lots and added one at a time, only as the price moves in your favor:

Lot12345
Added when price is…at entry+3%+6%+10%+14%
Share of the position35%25%20%12%8%

Only Lot 1 (35%) goes on at entry. The other four are "lot triggers" that fire automatically as the trade climbs — so the position grows biggest only when the trade is most clearly working, and tapers off near the top. Each added lot updates the average cost. Shorts mirror this downward.

Keeping the position inside 10% — the lots adjust themselves

Because the later lots fill at higher prices, a full five-lot position could drift past the 10%-of-NAV ceiling. Ambush prevents this automatically. Every minute it re-does the lot math against the current NAV (so the plan grows or shrinks as the account does). If adding the next lot would push the stock past 10% of NAV, the engine trims shares from the top lots first — Lot 5, then Lot 4 — keeping the position at or under 10% and, as a bonus, improving the average cost (it keeps more of the cheaper early shares and fewer of the expensive top ones). The position cards on the Ambush screen show these adjusted share counts as they change. This is the same proven method already used by the fund's other order engine; Ambush carries its own copy so it never depends on anything outside itself.

Costs are always included: a small slippage estimate on every entry and exit, the broker's per-share commission, and borrow cost on shorts.

6. The life of a trade — the six stages

On the dashboard, a stock flows down through six boxes. It's grey while it waits in a box, and turns green the moment it fires that stage. If a trade closes, the name drops back and waits to go green again.

StageWhat it means
① STALKINGThe waiting pool — every stock that currently has a weekly Buy-Long or Sell-Short signal. Watching for the daily breakout.
② HUNTINGThe daily breakout has cleared today. Now watching, minute by minute, for the hourly trigger.
③ ATTACKThe hourly trigger just fired. The order goes out on the next tick.
④ DEVOURA real, confirmed position is held. The protective stop is live at the broker. (Behind the scenes there's a brief "order sent, not yet confirmed" step that's hidden from view — see the safety net.)
⑤ STILL HUNGRYA stock that was held and has since exited, now hunting another entry (a "re-entry"). Shows the price it has to hold to stay eligible.
⑥ PROTECTThe trailing stop has climbed above the average cost — the trade can no longer lose money. The profit is locked.
7. A brand-new entry — step by step
  1. Is it eligible? The weekly signal is on and the sector is not "AVOID."
  2. Is the daily breakout intact right now? The live price is above the prior two-day high (long) or below the prior two-day low (short).
  3. Did the hourly trigger just fire? The live price broke the most recent completed hourly bar's high (long) or low (short) by a penny.
  4. Set the stop and the size. For a brand-new position the protective stop is the low of the first hour of trading (9:30–10:30), minus a small fee, for a long — the "disaster floor." (For a short it's the first-hour high plus a fee.) The risk-per-share is the entry price minus that stop, and the position is sized as in Section 5.
  5. Run the final safety checks. Is there enough cash for Lot 1? Is the position under its caps? Are we outside the no-entry windows? Does a fresh look at the broker confirm we currently hold none of this stock (never pile on top of an existing position)?
  6. Send it. The engine sends a market order for Lot 1 together with its protective stop, so the moment it fills it is already protected.
8. The exit — the two-bar hourly trailing stop

This is how Ambush protects a winner and gets out of a loser. For every held position, each minute it computes the two-bar level:

Long: stop = the lowest low of the last two completed hourly bars, minus a penny.
Short: stop = the highest high of the last two completed hourly bars, plus a penny.
  • It is a trailing stop: as the trend climbs, the two-bar low rises and the stop follows it up. It does not slide back down underneath you — if the price ever drops below the two-bar low, the stop has already fired and you're out, with the trade closed.
  • Whenever this level changes by a penny or more, the engine replaces the resting stop at the broker with the new, tighter one. The broker always holds exactly one protective stop at the correct level — and it will execute on its own even if the engine or the internet goes down.
  • The stop level is set from the last two completed hourly bars only — the still-forming current hour doesn't move where the level sits. But the exit itself is live: the instant the price drops below that level — even in the middle of the current hour, before it has closed — the resting stop fires and you're out. It does not wait for the current bar to close.
  • Once the stop has trailed up above your average cost, the position flips to PROTECT — from that point the trade cannot lose money.

There is also a belt-and-suspenders version: the resting stop at the broker is the primary protection, and if the engine sees on a tick that the price is already through the stop, it sends the exit itself. A check beforehand makes sure the broker still holds the position, so the two can never double-sell.

9. Getting back in — a re-entry

When a position exits — by the stop firing, by your hand, or by reconciliation — the stock does not disappear. It goes back to the waiting pool (STILL HUNGRY) and can be re-entered, but only if it passes a strict set of checks. The point of these checks is simple: only get back in if the trade is genuinely still working — never chase a stock that is rolling over.

  1. The weekly signal is still on. If the weekly Buy-Long / Sell-Short has ended, the stock is dropped entirely. (We don't need a separate weekly price check — the weekly fires long before the daily, so by the time the daily test below is met, the weekly is already satisfied.)
  2. The sector is not "AVOID."
  3. The price is still above the original DAILY breakout level. When the trade first fired this cycle, the daily breakout price — the 2-day-high level, for example $463.89 — was frozen and is held. It is not re-figured to a new level each day. Re-entry requires the price to still be above that frozen daily level. If it falls back below, the daily uptrend that defined the trade is fading, so the stock is not eligible; the moment it climbs back above, it's eligible again. This is what keeps us from re-buying a trade that's rolling over. Important: this frozen rule applies to the daily level only — not the weekly (which is just a signal, no price level) and not the hourly.
  4. The hourly trigger fires again — the live price breaks the high of the most recent completed hourly bar (long) / its low (short). This is a fresh trigger every time, and it is perfectly fine — in fact, welcome — if it fires at a lower price than your last entry. As long as you're still above the frozen daily floor, a pullback that then breaks the prior hour's high lets you re-enter at a better, cheaper price. Same trigger as a new entry, any bar color.

The tighter stop on a re-entry

A re-entry is treated more carefully than a first entry, because you're jumping back into a trade mid-move and want quick proof that the momentum is real:

  • The stop starts at the low of the entry bar — the very hourly bar during which the price broke back through. This is a tight stop.
  • If the price dips below that bar's low, the engine exits immediately with a very small loss. The stock is then free to re-enter again the next time it breaks the previous bar's high — no waiting period.
  • If the price stays above that low, the momentum is confirmed and you're holding a winner you got into early.
  • This tight entry-bar stop is held through the entry bar and the next two completed hourly bars. After that, there are enough bars to compute the normal two-bar hourly trailing stop, and the position switches over to that — the same trailing exit as any other held trade.

A brand-new entry, by contrast, starts with the wider first-hour low as its stop (Section 7), not the entry-bar low. That first-hour low stays the stop while it is still the lowest of the last two completed hourly bars. Once two more hourly bars have formed and the first hour ages out of that two-bar window, the stop becomes the lowest low of the most recent two bars — higher, in a rising trend — and from there it trails upward, hour by hour, like any other position. The tight entry-bar stop is for re-entries only.

11. What happens inside one 60-second tick
  1. Wake up and lock. Confirm it's market hours and the engine is on; grab the single-run lock; read the current account size and growth dial.
  2. Pull the live broker snapshot — your real positions and your real resting stops — as the source of truth.
  3. Confirm any order sent last tick. If the broker now shows the position, mark it as held. If it's still showing nothing after 3 minutes, the order never filled (rejected or lost), so the engine drops it back to waiting. (This 3-minute timeout is a built-in safety so a dropped order can never become a fake "held" position — see Section 13.)
  4. Adopt any hand-placed trade the engine isn't already managing (Section 12).
  5. Clean up phantoms — if the engine thinks it holds something but the broker shows zero, clear it. The engine's books always converge to the broker.
  6. Get live prices and roll each stock's latest price into its hourly bar.
  7. Manage held positions — add lots (with the 10% trim), trail the stop, take exits.
  8. Handle re-entries — fire the ones that just triggered and watch the rest.
  9. Look for brand-new entries, then save all state and send any orders to the broker.
12. Adopting your own hand-placed trades

If you place a trade yourself in the broker, Ambush notices a position it isn't managing and adopts it: it treats the shares you hold as Lot 1, sets the two-bar hourly trailing stop, and arms Lots 2 through 5 to add as the trade climbs — exactly as if the engine had entered it. From that point your manual trade is managed like any other.

14. One full trade, start to finish
  1. Monday's weekly close puts stock XYZ into a Buy-Long setup. It enters the waiting pool.
  2. Wednesday, 11:10 AM: XYZ is trading above its prior two-day high (daily breakout intact), and the live price ticks above the 10:00–11:00 bar's high. Trigger.
  3. 11:11 AM: the engine sizes Lot 1 (say 35 shares at the 50% dial), sets the stop at the first-hour low, and sends the buy plus the stop.
  4. 11:12 AM: the broker confirms 35 shares. The position is now held, the stop resting at the broker.
  5. The price climbs +3% and +6% — Lots 2 and 3 fill, and the average cost updates. The two-bar hourly stop trails up behind the price; once it passes the average cost, XYZ flips to PROTECT and can no longer lose money. (If a later lot would push the stock past 10% of NAV, the engine trims the top lots to keep it inside the cap.)
  6. Thursday, 2:00 PM: the price pulls back through the two-bar low. The resting stop fills the exit; the engine logs the trade and drops XYZ back to the waiting pool.
  7. Thursday, 3:10 PM: XYZ's weekly signal is still on, and the price is still above the original breakout price that first qualified it. It breaks the prior hour's high again — re-enter, this time with the tight entry-bar-low stop. The cycle repeats, right up to the 4:00 close.

16. What changed in this revision (V7.4 → V7.5)
  1. Re-entry now uses the original, frozen breakout DAILY price. A stock can only re-enter while the DAILY price holds above the original DAILY price that first qualified the trade — not a price that resets each day. If it falls below that, we don't chase it down. (This is the daily level only — not the hourly.)
  2. Re-entries get a tight entry-bar stop. A re-entry's stop starts at the low of the bar it broke in on; dip below it and we're out for a tiny loss; hold it for two bars, then the normal two-bar hourly stop takes over. (Brand-new entries keep the wider first-hour-low stop.)
  3. The entry trigger is a plain break of the prior hourly bar's high/low — no requirement that the bar be a particular color.
  4. The five-lot plan now re-balances itself every minute against the current NAV, trimming the top lots so a position never exceeds 10% of NAV and the average cost stays as low as possible.
  5. The first-hour wording is corrected: existing positions are protected and can be stopped out in the first hour; only new positions and stop re-tightening wait until 10:30.
  6. The sector filter is fixed and made directional. It was checking for a label ("AVOID") that the system never actually assigns, so it was doing nothing — every sector passed, and the old backtest had no sector filtering at all. It is now the simple BULL/BEAR rule: a long is only taken in a sector that's positive over the last 5 days, a short only in one that's negative. The new backtest will reflect real sector filtering.

These changes will be confirmed by a fresh backtest before any of them go live. Once you sign off on this document, the backtest runs; if the numbers hold up, the live engine is updated to match this exactly.
