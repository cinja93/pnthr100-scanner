1. What Ambush is, in one paragraph

Ambush is an intraday momentum engine that trades the AI 300 universe (~300 names). It does not invent its own opinion of a stock. Instead it waits for three things to line up — a weekly trend signal, a daily breakout, and an hourly trigger — and only then takes a position, in the direction of the trend. It pyramids into winners in five lots, trails a stop two bars behind price, and exits the moment price breaks that trailing level. It manages risk per trade to a hard dollar cap, reconciles its own book against your live IBKR account every minute, and treats your IBKR account as the single source of truth. It only trades while the market is open, and it never opens a new position during the first hour or the brief open blackout — but it now takes new entries right up to the 4:00 closing bell.

2. The heartbeat — how often it thinks
  • Every 60 seconds, Monday–Friday, while the market is open (9:30 AM – 4:05 PM ET). A scheduler fires every minute; the engine itself refuses to run outside 9:30–16:05 ET or on weekends.
  • One tick at a time. A database lock guarantees only one server instance can process a given minute — so a deploy, autoscale, or stray server can never double-process and double an order.
  • One designated writer. Only the instance with the writer flag set actually ticks; everything else stays silent. This is why there's exactly one engine acting on the account.
  • If Ambush is turned off in its config, or it's outside hours, the tick exits immediately and does nothing.

Each minute the engine does the same nine-step sequence (Section 7). Between ticks it does nothing — all state lives in the database, so it survives restarts.

3. The signal stack — what has to line up before it enters

Think of three filters stacked on top of each other. A name must pass all three, at the present moment, to be entered. This is the heart of the system.

Layer 1 — WEEKLY: "is this a valid trend setup?"

Computed once from weekly bars using a sector-tuned moving average (the "OpEMA", roughly 18–30 weeks depending on the sector). A name is in an active Buy-Long (BL) setup when, on the weekly chart: price closed above a rising average, broke the prior two weeks' high, and the pullback low sat in a "daylight zone" just above the average (about 1%–25% above it) — i.e. a healthy uptrend that isn't overextended. Sell-Short (SS) is the mirror image below a falling average. The weekly signal stays "active" until a weekly exit (a break of the two-week low for longs). The engine checks is this week inside an active BL/SS window? — that's the weekly gate.

Plain version: the weekly signal decides which ~handful of the 300 names are even eligible today, and in which direction. Everything else is timing.

Layer 2 — DAILY: "is the breakout intact right now?"

The engine pulls the last two daily bars and requires the current price to be above the higher of the prior two days' highs (for a long) or below the lower of the prior two days' lows (for a short), plus a penny. This is the daily breakout, checked against the live price. If price falls back inside that 2-day range, the daily breakout is no longer intact and the name is not a candidate this minute.

Layer 3 — HOURLY: "has it just triggered?"

The precise tripwire. The engine takes the most recent completed hourly bar and waits for the live price to break its high by a penny (long) / its low by a penny (short). This is an N=1, 1-bar live break — it fires the instant price crosses, on the 60-second tick, not after a bar closes. That break is the entry.

Regime & sector, two side-filters: A market-regime read (the PAI300 index vs its 36-week average) is computed but the gate is turned off in V7.4 — longs and shorts are taken in any regime. A sector filter is active: if a name's sector is ranked "AVOID," it's skipped. So the full eligibility test is: weekly signal + sector not-AVOID + daily breakout + hourly trigger.
4. Money — sizing, risk, and the five lots

How big a position gets

For every entry the engine computes the risk per share = entry price − stop price (long). Then total shares = the smallest of three caps:

CapFormulaMeans
Max dollar risk$300 ÷ risk-per-shareNever risk more than $300 on one trade
1% of NAV risk(1% × NAV) ÷ risk-per-shareRisk ≤ 1% of the account per trade
10% ticker cap(10% × NAV) ÷ entry priceNo single name > 10% of NAV

That share count is then multiplied by a graduated sizing factor based on account size, so risk stays proportional as the fund grows:

NAVSizingEffect
Below $125K50%~$150 max risk per trade (current tier)
$125K – $166K75%~$225 max risk
$166K and up100%full $300 max risk

The five-lot pyramid

The total share count is split into five lots: 35% / 25% / 20% / 12% / 8%. Only Lot 1 (35%) goes on at entry. The other four are "lot triggers" that fire as price moves in your favor, at these distances above the original entry (for a long):

Lot12345
Trigger (above entry)entry (0%)+3%+6%+10%+14%
Share share35%25%20%12%8%

So it adds the most size early and tapers off — building a bigger position only as the trade proves itself. Each add updates the average cost. Shorts mirror this downward.

Costs baked in: 5 basis points of slippage on every entry and exit, $0.005/share IBKR commission, and borrow cost on shorts.

7. What happens inside one 60-second tick (the 9 steps)
  1. Wake & lock. Confirm it's market hours, Ambush is enabled, and grab the one-tick lock. Read NAV from your IBKR-synced account size; compute the sizing tier.
  2. Load the live IBKR snapshot — your real positions and your real resting stops — as the source of truth.
  3. Confirm FILLING entries. Any order sent last tick: if IBKR now shows the position → promote to ACTIVE; if still flat after 3 minutes → it never filled, revert to STALKING. (Section 8.)
  4. Auto-adopt. Any position IBKR holds that the engine isn't managing → adopt it (give it a 2-bar stop + a pyramid plan). Covers manual trades. (Section 11.)
  5. Reconcile phantoms. Any position the engine thinks it holds but IBKR shows flat → clear it to STALKING. The book always converges to IBKR. (Section 11.)
  6. Get live prices (IBKR for held names, a market-data quote for the rest) and roll each name's 60-second price into its hourly bar.
  7. Manage held positions (Phase A): pyramids, trailing stop, exits. (Sections 8–9.) Also freezes/refreshes each name's Weekly + Daily Trigger levels.
  8. Process triggered re-entries (Phase B) and watch for new re-entry triggers (Phase C).
  9. Scan for brand-new entries (Phase D), then save all state and send any orders to the bridge.
8. ENTRIES — every scenario

8a. A brand-new entry (the main case)

This is a name the engine isn't already in. Walk the stack:

  1. Eligible? Weekly BL/SS active + sector not "AVOID."
  2. Daily breakout intact? Live price above the prior 2-day high (long) / below the prior 2-day low (short).
  3. Hourly trigger? Live price breaks the most-recent completed hourly bar's high (long) / low (short) by a penny.
  4. Stop & size. Stop = the first-hour low minus a fee (long) / first-hour high plus a fee (short). Risk-per-share = entry − stop. Size the position (Section 4). If the stop is on the wrong side of entry or the math doesn't work, skip.
  5. Final safety checks. Enough cash to fund Lot 1? Under the position cap? Not in a blackout window? A fresh IBKR snapshot confirms we're currently flat in this name (never stack on top of an existing position)?
  6. Send it. The engine writes the name as FILLING (0 shares — not yet "held") and sends a market BUY/SELL for Lot 1 plus its protective stop, together, to IBKR.

8b. The fill confirmation (why phantoms can't happen)

A position is never marked "held" on hope. After sending the order:

  • Next tick, if IBKR shows the position on the right side → promote to ACTIVE using the real fill size and price. The stop is already in place from step 6.
  • If the order was rejected or never filled (blackout, buying power, a halt, a dropped order) → IBKR stays flat → after 3 minutes the engine reverts it to STALKING. No fake "held" position is ever left behind.
Net effect: "ACTIVE" in the engine now requires IBKR to confirm the shares exist. A rejected order can't become a phantom — it just quietly goes back to watching.

8c. A re-entry (after an exit)

When a position exits — by your hand, by its stop firing, or by reconciliation — it does not disappear. It drops to STALKING and is treated as a fresh candidate. To re-enter, it must pass the same gates as a new entry, evaluated right now:

  • Weekly BL/SS still active (if the weekly signal has expired, the name is dropped entirely).
  • Sector not "AVOID."
  • Holds above both frozen triggers (the rule added tonight — see the box below).
  • Daily breakout still intact at the present price (the 2-day high/low test). If the daily has reversed, it waits — it is not a candidate this minute, but it stays STALKING and keeps checking.
  • Then the 1-bar hourly live break triggers it.
The frozen-trigger re-entry gate (new tonight). When a name first trades a cycle, the engine freezes two price levels and keeps them until the weekly signal resets:
  • Weekly Trigger — the breakout price the active weekly BL/SS signal fired at (e.g. 420.51).
  • Daily Trigger — the original 2-day breakout level that first fired this cycle (e.g. 463.89).
A long can only re-enter while live price is at or above both; a short only while at or below both. The check is stateless every tick: if price dips under either level it waits (it does not chase), and if it later reclaims both it's eligible again. If a trigger isn't frozen yet, the engine falls back to today's rolling 2-day level — it never re-enters blind. Both levels show as columns on the dashboard.

When it triggers, it goes ATTACK → the next tick sends the order → FILLING → DEVOUR, exactly like a new entry. There is no cooldown, no per-day cap, and no "already traded today" block — if the setup is valid and price holds both triggers, it re-enters.

8d. An adopted entry (your manual trades)

If you place a trade by hand, the engine sees a position in IBKR it isn't managing and adopts it: it sets a 2-bar trailing stop and builds a pyramid plan (lots 2–5), so your manual trade is managed exactly like an engine entry. (Details in Section 11.)

9. PYRAMIDING & EXITS — every scenario

9a. Adding to a winner (pyramiding)

While a position is ACTIVE/PROTECT, each tick checks the next lot trigger. When price reaches the next level (Section 4), the engine records the lot fill, updates the average cost, and places the order for that lot. It stops after Lot 5. Pyramiding never touches the protective stop — that's managed in one place only (next).

9b. The trailing stop (the primary exit) — "2-bar break"

This is the core exit. Every tick, for each held position, the engine computes the 2-bar level:

Long: stop = (lowest low of the last two completed hourly bars) − $0.01.
Short: stop = (highest high of the last two completed hourly bars) + $0.01.
  • This level tracks both up and down as new bars form — it is not a one-way ratchet. As the trend climbs, the two-bar low rises and the stop follows it up.
  • Whenever the level changes by a penny or more, the engine cancels and re-places a single resting stop order in IBKR at the exact new level. So IBKR always holds exactly one protective stop at the rule level — and it will execute the exit even if the engine or the bridge goes down.
  • Bars used are COMPLETED bars only. The bridge drops the still-forming current-hour bar, so "the last two bars" are always the two most recently finished hours.

9c. The engine's own exit + the resting stop (belt and suspenders)

There are two ways a position exits, and they agree:

  1. The resting IBKR stop fires on its own when price trades through the 2-bar level. This is the primary, always-on protection.
  2. The engine's fast-path: if on a tick the live price is already at/through the stop, the engine itself sends the exit (a market SELL/COVER), logs the trade with its P&L, and drops the name to STALKING.

A reconcile check prevents a double-exit: before sending any exit the engine verifies IBKR still holds the position; if IBKR is already flat (the resting stop beat it to it), the engine just cleans up its record instead of sending a second order.

9d. The "naked position" self-heal

Every tick the engine checks the live IBKR stop orders. If a held position has no protective stop in IBKR (e.g. an adopted position whose stop wasn't placed, or a stop that got cancelled), it places one immediately at the current 2-bar level — regardless of what its own record says. A held position is never left naked.

9e. Break-even snap — OFF in V7.4

An older rule that parked the stop at break-even once a trade was +$75 is disabled. The 2-bar trailing stop governs the exit from the moment of entry; the first-hour low is the disaster floor underneath it.

9f. Weekly signal expiry

If a STALKING name's weekly BL/SS signal ends (a weekly exit prints), the engine deletes it from the watch list — it's no longer a setup, so no re-entry. (A name that is currently held is still managed to its 2-bar stop regardless; the weekly expiry only removes watch-list names.)

10. The full entry-to-exit story (one trade, plain English)
  1. Monday's weekly close puts XYZ into a Buy-Long setup. It enters the watch list as STALKING.
  2. Wednesday 11:10 AM: XYZ is trading above its prior 2-day high (daily breakout intact) and the live price ticks above the 10:00–11:00 hourly bar's high. Trigger. → ATTACK.
  3. 11:11 AM: the engine sizes Lot 1 (say 35 shares at the 50% tier), sets the stop at the first-hour low, and sends a market BUY + the stop. → FILLING.
  4. 11:12 AM: IBKR confirms 35 shares filled. → DEVOUR (engine ACTIVE), stop resting in IBKR.
  5. Price climbs +3%, +6% — Lots 2 and 3 fire and load. Average cost updates. The 2-bar trailing stop climbs behind price; once it passes the average cost the name flips to PROTECT (can't lose now).
  6. Thursday 2:00 PM: price pulls back and trades through the 2-bar low. The resting stop fills the exit; the engine logs the trade and drops XYZ to STALKING.
  7. Thursday 3:10 PM: XYZ's weekly signal is still active and price is still above both its frozen Weekly and Daily Triggers. It breaks the prior hour's high again — re-enter. The whole cycle repeats, right up to the 4:00 close.

12. Things to watch / questions to sanity-check before the open
  1. First-hour exits. Between 9:30 and 10:30 the engine doesn't recompute trailing stops or run exit logic — it leans on the resting IBKR stop. If a name gaps hard at the open, only that resting stop protects it until 10:30. Is that the behavior you want, or should the engine actively manage exits in the first hour too?
  2. "Daily" = the 2-day-high/low breakout. There is no separate daily moving-average signal in Ambush; the daily check is purely the 2-day range break.
  3. Re-entry has no cooldown. A whippy name that keeps breaking and failing could re-enter multiple times in a day (each time risking the per-trade cap), but it must reclaim both frozen triggers first. That's intentional per your rule, but worth eyeballing on a choppy day.
  4. Late-day entries (new tonight). Entries now fire right up to 4:00. A 3:55 PM breakout will be taken and held overnight with its resting stop — by design (institutional close momentum), but it means fresh overnight exposure put on minutes before the bell.
  5. Sizing tier is 50% at the current NAV — max risk ~$150/trade, not $300. It steps up only above $125K / $166K.
  6. Regime gate is OFF. Longs and shorts are taken in any market regime. (Intentional in V7.4.)
  7. Prices for non-held names come from a market-data quote feed, while held names use IBKR's price. A quote feed hiccup would only delay a new entry, never mismanage a held position (those use IBKR).
